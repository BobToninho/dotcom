What do you need to be a good people leader? (or, A fool believing he's saying something smart)

May 2026

From May 2025 to June 2026 I took on the role of Team Captain (TC) (at work, not at home (yet)). My team comprised of 5 software engineers and me, and it was newly formed in May, exactly when I took on the role. Me and the Product Owner (PO) were both completely new in the role. Recipe for disaster? I’m sure the thought crossed some people’s mind during those tumultuous days. But facts say there was no disaster. Instead, the team delivered its first new feature on time and picked up an entirely new nontrivial feature, which is set to be delivered on time while I’m writing this.

But I don’t want this blog post to be about delivery: I’d like to share my experience as a people leader, especially what I think should be done to be a good people leader. Because I took on the role for just over a year, my advice is to take all what I say with a grain of salt. Mastery isn’t developed in a year.

In the company I work with the team leadership role is split in two—Team Captain and Product Owner. The PO cares about delivery, the TC about the people. I can’t really speak for the job of the PO, if not from second hand experience.

※

The job of a people leader is very different to a software engineer’s. As a people leader, you can’t compile people from source code to machine code and you can’t use a debugger to find problems on them. Instead, you can talk to them and you can observe how they behave. After some time observing, you’ll know your team a little better than others do. You’ll start noticing patterns: person A likes thing X but hates thing Y, or person B has the habit of doing Z, but they didn’t know about it before you told them. With time, this knowledge builds to become your personal view on each of your team mates; a persona.

A note of caution: don’t force yourself into noticing patterns. Been there, done that. If one day you see person C not being interested in what you say and not being responsive it doesn’t mean that such traits are part of their persona. Maybe they are tired from the night before or they are just having a bad day. Instead, let time be your friend and filter out the outliers. After months of meeting your team every week you’ll have a draft of each persona in your mind, and you won’t even need notes for that.

※

Any fool can criticize, complain, and condemn—and most fools do. But it takes character and self-control to be understanding and forgiving.

—Dale Carnegie

People want to be heard and understood, but the exact opposite comes easier: ignore and pretend. When someone shares their feelings and opinions with you, they are doing it because it’s your job to listen to them and decide if taking action on what they shared with you. But it’s the sole thinking about this that makes it already worth it for them. Try to switch sides for a moment. Why would you care to share a feeling with someone that is supposed to take care of you? You would like them to remember and take action, or at least tell you why they didn’t take any action. This as a small step that is also easy to forget (can’t say I never did), but that it can make a difference. This is the definition of caring.

※

You’ll also have to be a little selfish, which may seem counterintuitive at first. Why being selfish if you have to care about the team? Being selfish helps you think what each your teammates want. Each and any of us is selfish (When we are not engaged in thinking about some definite problem, we usually spend about 95 percent of our time thinking about ourselves, Carnegie says), so avoiding going through hoops and simply thinking what you really want will help the whole team. Do you want to be treated more fairly by the management? So does your team. Do you want to be given enough fulfilling work? So does your team.

Of course, this calls for balance. There are things that are specific to you and someone else probably doesn’t want. You’ll develop a sense of what these things are by, surprise surprise, observing your team.

※

I hope this blog post doesn’t come off as proselytizing. So, what do you need to be a good people leader? My take: observe, listen, be selfish.
